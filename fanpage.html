Sonia Sotomayor

Justice. Activist. Latina. Leader. Feminist.

Why Sonia Sotomayor is a hero to me:

  • She is the First Hispanic Woman Supreme Court Justice and the third woman to serve on the highest court of the United States
  • She faced many obstacles, but never gave up
  • She fights passionately for causes she cares about
  • She works hard to achieve her dreams

Princeton

Sonia Sotomayor was born to Puerto Rican parents in the Bronx. She attended Princeton on a full schalorship due, in part, to affirmative action. She put in long hours of study because she did not fit in with the mostly white, upper-class student body. Her writing skills and vocabulary were not very good and she did not ask any questions her freshman year for fear of embarrassing herself. A professor helped her after hours and she gained skills, knowledge, and confidence.
Sotomayor was an advocate for other students and faculty. There were no full-time Latino faculty at Princeton so she met with the university president, but saw no results. She went on to file a formal letter of complaint and the university began to hire Latino faculty. She worked with a history professor to start a Puerto Rican history and politics seminar.
In 1976, Sotomayor graduated with high honors.

Yale Law School

Sonia Sotomayor was an activist throughout her academic career for other hispanic students and faculty. She was known as a hard worker, but not considered a start student in her class. During a recruiting dinner, a representative of a famous D.C. lawfirm suggested that she was only at Yale due to affirmative action. She filed a letter of complaint and did not accept a job offer with the firm. Her actions sparked a campus-wide debate. The firm's subsequent apology was published in the Washington Post.

Supreme Court

New York Senators Charles Schumer and Kirsten Gillibrand jointly urged President Obama to nominate Sotomayor to the position of Supreme Court Justice. She became only the second jurist to be nominated to three different judicial positions by three different presidents after Obama awarded her the nomination.
Sotomayor's first case was the controversial Citizens United v. Federal Election.
"What you are suggesting is that the courts, who created corporations as persons, gave birth to corporations as persons, and there could be an argument made that that was the Court's error to start with ... [imbuing] a creature of State law with human characteristics."
She dissented to the ruling that famously treated corporations as persons.
By the end of her fifth year on the court, Sotomayor had become especially visible in oral arguments and in passionate dissents from various majority rulings, especially those involving issues of race, gender and ethnic identity.
